The 6x data expansion fundamentally changes the statistical landscape of our Polymarket strategies -- but it does NOT change the structural arguments about where edge exists in prediction markets. The assessment is: more data will help us finally PROVE or DISPROVE our strategies with statistical rigor, but my prior expectation is that it will CONFIRM the negative assessment of pm-mean-reversion, show MIXED results for pm-information-edge, and strengthen the case for pm-correlation-pairs as the only consistently profitable automated approach. ## Question 1: Does the Assessment Change with 6x More Data?

### What Changes

**Statistical power increases dramatically.** With 31 days and 2-5 trades per market, we could not distinguish signal from noise. With 6-7 months:
- Mean reversion strategies on volatile markets could generate 15-40 trades per market instead of 2-5
- Momentum strategies could generate 8-20 trades instead of 1-3
- We cross the minimum threshold of 30 trades needed for basic statistical inference on at least some markets
- Walk-forward validation becomes possible for the first time (impossible before)

**Forward-fill artifacts decrease.** With windowed fetching at fidelity=60, we get roughly 1 real data point per hour. Over 6 months that is approximately 4,380 hourly candles, with perhaps 60-80% being real price observations (vs ~30-50% with the old method). This means:
- BB calculations use more real data, less stale forward-fill
- Volume=0 filtering works better (skipping synthetic candles)
- Indicator calculations are more meaningful

**Regime diversity improves.** 6-7 months covers multiple market phases:
- Pre-event quiet periods (low vol, flat)
- Information shock periods (high vol, directional)
- Resolution approach periods (price compression toward 0 or 1)
- This is critical -- a strategy that only works in one regime is useless

### What Does NOT Change

**Prediction market prices are still NOT traditional asset prices.** The fundamental argument from the previous assessment stands:
- Prices are bounded [0,1] probabilities reflecting beliefs about binary outcomes
- "Overbought" and "oversold" are the wrong mental model -- a move from 0.50 to 0.70 is new information, not a deviation from equilibrium
- Mean reversion only makes sense if the market OVERREACTS to information, which the academic literature (Restocchi et al. 2019, 6,058 markets, 8.4M price points) does find, but the effect is small and inconsistent
- Transaction costs (1% slippage) still eat into thin edges

**The edge hierarchy remains the same:**
1. Bundle/structural arbitrage (not feasible for us)
2. Market making (not feasible for us)
3. Favorite-longshot bias (partially feasible, needs system extension)
4. Domain expertise (not automatable)
5. Correlation pairs (our best strategy)
6. Momentum (weak but real in event-driven markets)
7. BB mean reversion (weakest, conflicts with PM price mechanics)

### My Expectations for Each Strategy

| Strategy | 31-Day Verdict | 6-Month Expectation | Confidence |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|------------|
| pm-mean-reversion | 6/15 profitable, Sharpe 1.23 avg | Will likely DEGRADE. More data = more failed mean reversion signals in information-driven markets. Expect <40% profitable across 100 markets. | 75% |
| pm-information-edge | 1/15 profitable, Sharpe 0.09 avg | Will SLIGHTLY improve. More data means the trend filter works better and generates more trades in genuinely trending markets. Expect 15-25% of markets profitable. | 60% |
| pm-correlation-pairs | Sharpe 3.2, near-zero DD | Will STAY strong but still limited by pair availability. More data enables better correlation estimation. Expect 50-70% of qualifying pairs profitable. | 80% |

### Why Mean Reversion Likely Gets WORSE, Not Better

This is counterintuitive but important. With 31 days, many markets were in the "quiet phase" where price DID fluctuate around a mean, creating the illusion of mean reversion. Over 6 months, most markets will experience at least one major information shock that permanently shifts the probability -- invalidating the mean. A BB strategy that enters when price touches the lower band after a large downward information move (new negative evidence about the event) will get destroyed as the price does NOT revert but instead stays at the new level or continues dropping.

The Restocchi et al. (2019) paper did find overreaction patterns across 6,058 prediction markets, but the correction effect was small and most of the profit came from specific types of markets (sports with sequential rounds). The generic BB approach is too blunt to capture this.

### Why Momentum Might Slightly Improve

With more data:
- The minPriceRange filter becomes more meaningful (6 months shows true trending vs flat markets)
- Information cascades have time to develop and be captured
- More trades per market = better parameter estimation
- Walk-forward can validate if in-sample momentum patterns persist OOS

But the fundamental problem remains: by the time an hourly candle closes showing an 8pp move, the information is already priced in. We are chasing stale signals.

## Question 2: Market Selection Criteria for Scanning 100 Markets

### Recommended Filter Pipeline

I recommend a 3-stage filter to go from 700+ active markets down to ~100 scan candidates.

#### Stage 1: API-Level Filters (Gamma API query)
```
active=true, closed=false
```
This gives us all active, non-closed markets. Expect 500-700+ results.

#### Stage 2: Programmatic Pre-Filters (before fetching CLOB data)

| Filter | Threshold | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Volume | > $50,000 lifetime | Ensures real liquidity. Markets with <$50K volume have no reliable price discovery. |
| Age | > 60 days old | Must have enough history for windowed data fetch. Markets <60 days old will have <2 months of hourly data. |
| Resolution date | > 14 days from now | Markets about to resolve have compressed probabilities and are untradeable by technical strategies. |
| Exclude short-term | No 4h, 15m, 1h markets | Crypto price prediction markets with ultra-short resolution are noise. |
| Exclude resolved | Skip closed/resolved | Cannot trade resolved markets. |
| Category diversity | Include all categories | Do NOT filter by category -- we want diversity for generalizability testing. |

Expected output: ~200-300 markets.

#### Stage 3: CLOB Data Quality Filters (after fetching discovery data)

| Filter | Threshold | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Real data points | > 500 hourly points | Minimum for meaningful strategy testing. 500 points = ~21 days of dense data. |
| Data span | > 90 days | Ensures at least 3 months of history for train/test split. |
| Price range | NOT stuck at 0-5% or 95-100% | Markets stuck at extremes have no trading opportunity. Check if max-min price range > 5%. |
| Non-trivial volatility | Standard deviation of prices > 0.02 | Markets with std < 2% are dead/consensus markets. No signals possible. |

Expected output: ~100-150 markets.

#### Final Selection: Top 100 by Composite Score

Rank the filtered markets by:
```
score = (0.4 * normalized_volume) +
        (0.3 * normalized_data_density) +
        (0.2 * normalized_price_range) +
        (0.1 * days_to_resolution_factor)
```

Where:
- `normalized_volume` = log(volume) / max(log(volume))
- `normalized_data_density` = real_pts / total_hours_in_span
- `normalized_price_range` = (max_price - min_price) / 0.8 (capped at 1.0)
- `days_to_resolution_factor` = min(days_to_resolution, 180) / 180

Take the top 100 by this score.

### Category Distribution Target

For robust conclusions, aim for category diversity:
- Politics: 25-35 markets (largest pool on Polymarket)
- Crypto/Markets: 15-20 markets (high volume but often short-duration)
- Sports: 10-15 markets (if available with enough history)
- Science/Tech: 10-15 markets
- World Events/Geopolitics: 15-20 markets
- Entertainment/Culture: 5-10 markets

This diversity is important because different categories have different price dynamics:
- Politics: slow-moving, information-driven, long time horizons
- Crypto: correlated with crypto markets, shorter horizons, more technical
- Sports: binary with known event dates, potentially more mean-reverting
- Geopolitics: event-driven shocks, good for momentum

## Question 3: Parameter Strategy for Scanning

### Recommended Approach: Default Scan First, Then Selective Optimization

**Phase 1: Broad Scan with Default Parameters (100 markets)**

Run all 100 markets with the current production defaults for each strategy:

- pm-mean-reversion: bbPeriod=20, bbStdDev=2.0, exitStdDev=0.5, minBBWidth=0.08, minProfitPct=4
- pm-information-edge: momentumPeriod=20, entryThreshold=0.08, exitThreshold=0.04, minPriceRange=0.15, minProfitPct=8

Why defaults first:
1. Establishes a baseline across all markets
2. Identifies which markets generate trades at all (the filter rate is critical)
3. Fast (no optimization overhead)
4. Shows true strategy robustness (a good strategy should work with reasonable defaults)

**Phase 2: Parameter Sensitivity on Top 20 (selective optimization)**

For the top 20 markets by Sharpe or return from Phase 1, run a compact grid search:

pm-mean-reversion (3 key params, ~27 combinations):
- bbStdDev: [1.5, 2.0, 2.5]
- minBBWidth: [0.04, 0.08, 0.12]
- minProfitPct: [2, 4, 6]

pm-information-edge (3 key params, ~27 combinations):
- entryThreshold: [0.05, 0.08, 0.12]
- minPriceRange: [0.08, 0.12, 0.18]
- minProfitPct: [4, 6, 8]

**Phase 3: Walk-Forward on Top 10**

For the top 10 markets from Phase 2, run proper walk-forward with 70/30 train/test split.

### Why NOT Multiple Param Sets Across All 100

Running even 3 param sets across 100 markets = 300 backtests per strategy = 600 total. Each backtest with 6 months of hourly data could take 30-120 seconds. That is 5-20 hours of compute. Grid search across all 100 is impractical. Save optimization for promising markets only.

## Question 4: Statistical Significance Thresholds

### Minimum Trade Count

With 6-7 months of hourly data, here are the thresholds:

| Trades | Statistical Power | Verdict |
|--------|------------------|---------|
| < 10 | Very weak | Cannot draw conclusions. Discard results. |
| 10-20 | Weak | Directionally informative but not reliable. Note as "tentative." |
| 20-30 | Moderate | Minimum for basic inference. Can compute t-test for mean return != 0. |
| 30-50 | Good | Sufficient for initial strategy assessment. p < 0.05 test is meaningful. |
| 50+ | Strong | Robust conclusions possible. Walk-forward results are trustworthy. |

**For this scan: Require minimum 10 trades to include a market in results. Flag markets with 10-30 trades as "low confidence." Only draw firm conclusions from markets with 30+ trades.**

### Performance Thresholds

| Metric | "Noise" | "Interesting" | "Actually Works" |
|--------|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Sharpe Ratio | < 0.3 | 0.3 - 1.0 | > 1.0 |
| Total Return | < 2% | 2% - 10% | > 10% |
| Win Rate | < 45% | 45% - 55% | > 55% |
| Max Drawdown | > 20% | 10% - 20% | < 10% |
| Profit Factor | < 1.1 | 1.1 - 1.5 | > 1.5 |

**Key insight: A strategy that is "Interesting" on 50+ markets is more valuable than one that "Actually Works" on 3 markets.** The former suggests a real (if small) edge; the latter is likely overfitting.

### Statistical Validation Method

For each market result with 20+ trades, compute:
1. **t-statistic**: t = mean_return / (std_return / sqrt(n_trades))
2. **p-value**: One-tailed test (H0: mean return <= 0)
3. **Confidence interval**: 95% CI for mean trade return
4. **Aggregate statistics**: Across all 100 markets, what fraction are profitable? What is the aggregate Sharpe?

A strategy "actually works" if:
- More than 30% of markets are profitable (better than random)
- The aggregate Sharpe across all markets > 0.3
- At least 5 individual markets have Sharpe > 1.0 with 20+ trades
- Walk-forward OOS degradation < 50% on top markets

## Question 5: Pairs Discovery with Correlation Matrix

### Yes, Absolutely Include This

With 100 markets cached, computing a correlation matrix is one of the highest-value analyses we can do. Here is the plan:

**Step 1: Compute Pairwise Correlations**

For all 100 markets, compute pairwise Pearson correlation of hourly close prices over the maximum overlapping period. This produces a 100x100 correlation matrix with 4,950 unique pairs.

**Step 2: Filter for High Correlation**

| Correlation | Classification | Action |
|-------------|---------------|--------|
| > 0.95 | Very high | Prime candidates. Likely same-event different-deadline markets. |
| 0.85-0.95 | High | Good candidates. May be related events. |
| 0.70-0.85 | Moderate | Unlikely to work for pairs trading (spread too noisy). |
| < -0.85 | Anti-correlated | Interesting for reverse-pairs (buy one, short the other on divergence). |

**Step 3: Validate Pairs**

For each pair with correlation > 0.85:
- Check they are RELATED (same event, different thresholds or deadlines) -- not spuriously correlated
- Compute the spread (A - beta*B where beta = OLS hedge ratio)
- Test spread stationarity (ADF test or equivalent)
- If stationary, compute half-life of mean reversion
- Only trade pairs with half-life of 5-50 hours (sweet spot for hourly strategy)

**Step 4: Backtest pm-correlation-pairs**

Run pm-correlation-pairs on all qualifying pairs with 6 months of data. This is the most promising analysis in the entire scan.

### Implementation Note

The correlation matrix computation should be a standalone script that:
1. Loads the 100 market price series from cached CLOB data
2. Aligns them by timestamp (inner join on timestamps)
3. Computes correlation for each pair with minimum 200 overlapping data points
4. Outputs top pairs sorted by correlation
5. Saves results to a file for pm-correlation-pairs backtesting

## Question 6: New Strategies to Consider

### Strategy A: Overreaction Corrector (NEW -- Research-Backed)

**Research Basis**: Restocchi et al. (2019, European Journal of Operational Research) analyzed 6,058 prediction markets with 8.4 million price points and found systematic overreaction: "increasing and decreasing odds lead, respectively, to under- and over-estimation of odds-implied probabilities." Their correction methodology "considerably improved forecast quality."

**Adaptation for Our System**: Instead of generic BB mean reversion, implement a CONDITIONAL mean reversion that only triggers after detecting a likely overreaction pattern:

1. Detect a sharp price move (> 10pp in 6 hours)
2. Check if the move was driven by a BURST of trading (volume spike) -- suggesting emotional/reactive trading
3. Enter the opposite direction with the expectation of partial reversion
4. Exit at 50% reversion or after 24 hours

This is fundamentally different from BB mean reversion because:
- It targets SPECIFIC overreaction events, not general deviation from mean
- The volume filter distinguishes information-driven moves (no reversion expected) from panic/excitement-driven moves (reversion likely)
- The time limit prevents holding through genuine information shifts

**Feasibility**: Can be built with current system capabilities. Needs hourly data with meaningful volume proxy (which we now have with point counts).

**Priority**: MEDIUM -- Worth testing with 6 months of data, but the volume proxy (point count) may not be a reliable indicator of emotional vs informational trading.

### Strategy B: Favorite Compounder (PREVIOUSLY RECOMMENDED)

**Status**: I previously recommended building this. With 6 months of data, we can now BACKTEST it, which we could not do with 31 days.

**Backtesting Approach**:
- Scan for markets that WERE at 85-95% probability 30+ days before resolution
- Track whether they resolved YES
- Calculate the return: (1.00 - entry_price) / entry_price
- Compare win rate to entry probability (the edge is the DIFFERENCE)

**System Gap**: The backtesting engine does not natively support "hold to resolution" logic. We would need to either:
(a) Use the existing entry/exit framework with a "hold until resolution date" exit condition
(b) Build a dedicated resolution-aware backtester

**Priority**: HIGH -- This is the strategy with the strongest empirical backing. But it may not need the existing backtesting infrastructure at all. A simple analysis script might be better.

### Strategy C: Event Volatility Breakout (NEW)

**Concept**: Instead of trying to predict DIRECTION in prediction markets, trade VOLATILITY EXPANSION. When a market transitions from low-volatility consensus to high-volatility uncertainty, trade the breakout.

**Logic**:
1. Measure rolling volatility (BB width or standard deviation of closes)
2. When volatility contracts below threshold (market in consensus), set breakout levels
3. Enter LONG if price breaks above upper level, SHORT if below lower level
4. Exit on volatility contraction or mean reversion

**Why This Might Work**:
- Captures the "regime transition" in prediction markets
- Not directionally biased
- Works well with bounded [0,1] prices
- Academic analogy: Bollinger Band squeeze breakout, well-documented in equities

**Priority**: MEDIUM -- Interesting concept but unproven in prediction markets.

### Strategy D: Resolution Convergence (NEW)

**Concept**: As a market approaches its resolution date, the price must converge to either 0 or 1. Markets that are currently at 0.4-0.6 with resolution in 14-30 days will experience maximum volatility as the final information arrives. Trade the directional move during this convergence period.

**Logic**:
1. Calculate days to resolution
2. When resolution is 7-30 days away and price is 0.3-0.7, enter the prevailing trend direction
3. Use tight stops (the risk is a direction reversal near resolution)
4. Exit at 0.85+ or 0.15- (or at resolution)

**Priority**: LOW -- Requires resolution date awareness in the backtesting engine. Also, the "prevailing trend" in the final weeks is essentially a bet on the outcome, which is a domain expertise play, not a technical strategy.

### Recommendation: Focus on These for the Scan

1. **pm-correlation-pairs** (existing, highest priority)
2. **pm-information-edge** (existing, default params scan)
3. **pm-mean-reversion** (existing, default params scan -- expect negative results)
4. **Overreaction Corrector** (NEW, build and include in scan)
5. **Favorite Compounder backtest** (NEW, separate analysis script)

Do NOT build the Event Volatility Breakout or Resolution Convergence strategies for this scan. Save them for a future iteration after we have results from the existing strategies.

## Question 7: Walk-Forward Feasibility and Split Ratios

### Walk-Forward Is Now Feasible

With 6+ months of hourly data, walk-forward validation is finally possible. This is the single most important improvement the data upgrade enables.

### Recommended Split Configuration

**Simple Train/Test Split (Phase 3 of scan):**

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Train ratio | 0.70 | 70% train, 30% test. Standard in academic literature. |
| Min train bars | 2,000 (~83 days) | Ensures enough data for indicator warmup + parameter estimation. |
| Min test bars | 800 (~33 days) | Ensures enough bars for OOS trades to accumulate. |

For a market with 5,000 hourly data points (208 days):
- Train: bars 0-3,500 (146 days, ~6 months)
- Test: bars 3,500-5,000 (62 days, ~2 months)

**Why 70/30 and NOT 50/50 or 80/20:**
- 50/50: Too little training data. With hourly PM data and forward-fills, the effective usable data in the training set might be only 50-60% of the nominal bars. A 50% train window would leave too few real data points for parameter estimation.
- 80/20: Too little test data. With 5,000 bars, 20% = 1,000 bars. But with low trade frequency (maybe 1-2 trades per week), that is only 8-15 trades in the test set -- not enough for significance.
- 70/30: Best compromise. 3,500 train bars (ample for indicators and parameter search) + 1,500 test bars (room for 15-30 trades at reasonable frequency).

**Anchored Walk-Forward (for robustness, optional):**

For the very best-performing strategies from the scan, consider a 3-window anchored walk-forward:

| Window | Train | Test |
|--------|-------|------|
| 1 | Months 1-3 | Month 4 |
| 2 | Months 1-4 | Month 5 |
| 3 | Months 1-5 | Months 6-7 |

Aggregate OOS performance across all 3 test windows. This is more robust than a single split but requires markets with 5+ months of data.

### Walk-Forward Success Criteria

| Metric | Threshold | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| OOS Sharpe | > 0.3 | Lower bar than crypto because PM strategies trade less frequently |
| OOS Degradation | < 60% | Higher tolerance because PM markets have inherently more noise |
| OOS Trades | >= 8 | Minimum for any inference (lower than 20 because PM trades are rare) |
| OOS Win Rate | > 40% | Above random chance for directional strategies |
| Train-Test Consistency | Profit factor direction preserved | If PF > 1 in-sample, must also be > 1 OOS |
